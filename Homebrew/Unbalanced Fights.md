# Unbalanced Fights

*Philosophy source: r/cyberpunkred — "GM Advice: Balancing Fights Is Unnecessary, Actually" (2040's Discussion)*  
*Server rules: Blue*

Guidance for running encounters without fair fight math, plus this server's **Unbalanced** gig tier. Complements (and for Unbalanced gigs, overrides) the threat-weight tables in [GM Rules](GM%20Rules.md).

---

## Prerequisites (table / philosophy)

Use the philosophy below only if **all three** are true at your table:

1. You and your players have run Cyberpunk RED combat a few times and share a baseline understanding of it.
2. Your players can recognize fights they can't win.
3. There is trust that any proposed action will be adjudicated fairly.

If any of those are missing, stick to the balance guidelines in [GM Rules](GM%20Rules.md) and do not run **Unbalanced**.

## Server Rules — Unbalanced Tier

### What it is

A new gig difficulty: **Unbalanced**.

You are not building a fair fight. You run what the world would actually put in the way — then seriously engage any PC plan for getting through it (or around it, or out of it).

### Statblocks

- Keyed / named / custom opposition goes through that channel.
- **Mooks are mooks** — corebook (or existing approved) Mook blocks are fine without extra ceremony.

Loot / payout caps from normal Street/Merc difficulty tables **do not** apply the same way — pay what the fiction supports. Still convert one-off gig gear to RAW/server-approved items by end of gig per [GM Rules](GM%20Rules.md) loot policy unless staff says otherwise.

### Running Unbalanced

- **Premise must be descriptive.** You are a Fixer advertising to edgerunners. They will not take the job if they cannot tell what is in it.
- **Several paths must exist.** Some paths can be certain death — that is fine — but every option has to be reasonable lorewise. Do not leave only one "correct" combat solution.

### Play examples (signal failure early)

| Player choice | World response |
|---------------|----------------|
| Charge a corp building head-on | You're not V. The whole building's security shows up and they've called the cops. Stealth, soft entry, or getting the fuck out are also options. |
| Kill a bunch of mooks then take the elevator | Sleep-gas elevator. Once you're high enough, they cut the cables — elevator is cheaper than their lives. Try the stairs. |
| Hunker down and fight the cops | They do not stop. More units, heavier arms, until they get you. Run. |

Signal unwinnable fights clearly through the fiction. Be fair with dice. Reward clever play. Dumb decisions still stick.

## The Advice (philosophy)

Cyberpunk RED's rough fight math (1 Mook per PC, 1 Lieutenant per 2 PCs, a Boss vs a whole party) is useful as a baseline for "a fair fight."

No fight in Cyberpunk should ever be fair. Bad guys never give you one — even Adam Smasher rolls deep with mooks.

**Throw out balanced combat encounters. Run the world.**

Put obstacles equal to what would realistically be there. Then seriously engage any PC idea for solving them. That means:

- Give them a hearing
- Tell them information they can easily access (including consequences)
- Let player skill and luck decide the outcome
- Modify DVs for ingenuity, skill, and sheer chutzpah

### Example: The Scavs

The PCs just dropped a geared-up corpo hit team. They're at ~75% health and armor, stripping corpses for EQ rifles, special ammo, combat drugs. Then manhole covers scrape open. Roughly **forty scavs** appear under sodium light. They've left the PCs a clear exit back the way they came.

The scavs are armed but not yet aggressive — cautious, wary. Their leader is a five-foot-nothing girl named **Wilhemina**, armed only with a parasol, name tag still on an irreparably filthy Donut King uniform.

**Signal:** normal combat will not win this. Other exits exist.

| Approach | How to run it |
|----------|----------------|
| **Negotiate** | Scavs want loot, not a fight. PCs may take a few guns or clips; not most of it. Trading / Streetwise settles the split. |
| **Grab and run** | Scavs won't pursue. That's why the exit is open. |
| **Call in a favor** | Friend (or friend of a friend) of the scavs might buy the whole haul for a later favor — new adventure bait. |
| **Intimidate / Facedown** | Crowds: call out their best. One larger scav steps up (Boosterganger). Facedown with **+3** for numbers. Win → scavs get nervous and talk from a weaker position. |
| **Fight** | Scrupulously fair dice in the open. Don't stack every scav on round one (they're in each other's way). Merciful goals: scavs aren't here to murder — if PCs bail, they let them; if PCs go down, scavs stabilize them for chrome/gear harvest. TPK → wake with no chrome, no gear, bomb wired into skulls. New adventure. |
| **Clever escape** | Grapple guns off the street? Scavs wave as they go. Great speech? +1. Scav contact in a Life Path? If it makes sense, they're in the crowd. |

**Wilhemina:** Boosterganger stats, armed with a MiliTech Tactical Umbrella.

If the PCs somehow win (e.g. kill half the scavs and the rest bolt), let them own it — and remember: this scav gang now hates them.

### NPC Goals

Enemy goals must be **clear at the start** and should not change unless the players change them. Protean motivation makes the challenge impossible to read.

When placing a complication, know what it wants, why it's there, and how it got there. Keep reactions consistent.

If players are confused why something showed up, let the world explain it. Example: scavs talk — *"Hey, you guys done with those bodies? We can use all that gear… and protein!"* Closes the loop and invites the PCs into the scene.

### Beyond Combat

Same principle for social and investigative challenges. A MiliTech heiress buying out the cube hotel the PCs live in is not a balanced head-on fight — and shouldn't be. Leave space to innovate and let the players chart their own path.

### "If It Makes Sense"

Judgment call, broader than stats. Run complications only when the fiction supports them. Stay open to PC feedback when the fiction is unclear; clarify through the scene rather than OOC fiat.

### The Point

Balance only matters if you've decided the PCs have one allowed solution. Stay flexible, reward clever play, and you can drop almost anything in their lap — as long as it fits the world and the genre.

You're not trying to be a dick. Dumb decisions still have consequences.
